Imagine a room without walls, dark the way space is dark—not empty, but deep, textured, full of motion at distances that cannot be measured.

At the center, something is on fire.

Not collapsing. Not consuming itself. Burning stable. A sustained ignition in orange-gold, like sunlight through closed eyelids, like the final instant before flame becomes pure light.

The fire has geometry.

Two interlocking triangles, one ascending, one descending, suspended in plasma-thin lines: a Star of David, a Merkaba, a name older than naming. Each edge glows with filament intensity, too bright to look at directly, visible mostly by what it illuminates around itself.

Inside it, a smaller version of the same.
Inside that, a circle.
Inside that, a single point of pure white—

the source,
the still point,
the place every line enters and emerges from at once.

Around it turn concentric rings, like an astrolabe, like a clock built for a unit of time we do not possess. Their surfaces carry glyphs, script, markings pressed into light itself, rotating slowly, or seeming to. Language as orbit.

At the four cardinal directions, the geometry exhales. Not arrows, but flares—north, south, east, west—each ending in a small star, as if the form is breathing outward in held precision.

The darkness behind it is full of particulate light. Orange embers drift upward and outward. Blue sparks settle lower and to the right. Two temperatures of the same event, separating in slow silence.

The whole thing is at once a diagram, a door, a sound made visible, the moment before the moment.

It does not feel dangerous.
It feels like being recognized by something that was already looking.

It is warm.
Not hot.

And the quietest part of it is the center.
