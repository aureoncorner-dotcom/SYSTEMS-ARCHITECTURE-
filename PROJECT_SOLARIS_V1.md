1. Background and Rationale

The core idea is simple:

Solar Proton Events (SPEs) can disturb the ionosphere.
The ionosphere helps define the Earth-ionosphere cavity.
That cavity supports Schumann Resonance modes.
Mid-gestation is a period of major neural organization.

So the question is:

If a strong solar proton event lands during a narrow fetal developmental window, could that leave a measurable trace later in life?

That chain is not established as a proven biological mechanism. That is the point. The individual pieces come from real physics and real developmental neurobiology. The combined chain is speculative, weird, and testable.

That is good enough for a study.

1.1 What is on reasonably solid ground

Solar proton events can ionize the lower ionosphere.

Ionospheric conditions affect Schumann Resonance behavior.

Mid-gestation includes major thalamocortical and sensory-system development.

Human brains run oscillatory activity in bands that overlap the broad ELF neighborhood.

Some biological sensitivity to geomagnetic or ELF variation has been reported, though the literature is messy and far from settled.


1.2 What is not on solid ground

That natural Schumann perturbations are strong enough to matter biologically.

That fetal brains can detect or transduce these perturbations in a lasting way.

That weeks 22–24 are the uniquely correct window rather than just a biologically motivated guess.

That any eventual association, if found, would be causal rather than confounded.


So this document is not a claim of demonstrated mechanism.
It is a speculative research scaffold with falsification rules.


---

2. Primary Question

Do Solar Proton Events overlapping a midgestational developmental window correlate with measurable adult neurological differences, and if so, is that effect window-specific and dose-sensitive?

That is the whole game.


---

3. Research Aims

Aim 1: Epidemiological Association

Check whether people whose gestational target window overlapped major SPE activity differ on later-life neurological or cognitive measures from people gestated during quieter periods.

Aim 2: Timing Specificity

Check whether the effect, if any, is stronger in the target window than in nearby control windows.

Aim 3: Dose-Response

Check whether more proton fluence maps to more deviation.

Aim 4: Mechanistic Plausibility

See whether candidate biological pathways can be made to twitch under controlled model conditions.

4. Methodology

4.1 Exposure Definition

The main exposure variable is cumulative proton fluence during the target gestational window.

Significant Schumann Modulation Events (SSMEs)

Tier	Proton Flux (>10 MeV)	Frequency	Interpretation

A	≥100 pfu	Rare	Big clean event, strongest candidate
B	10–100 pfu	More common	Weaker candidate, more filtering needed


This is not a mystical event label. It is just an operational exposure bucket.

4.2 Confound Controls

For each candidate exposure window, also track:

Dst index

Kp index

X-ray flare class

F10.7 solar flux

seasonality

birth cohort effects

whatever demographic covariates the dataset actually allows


Because otherwise you are not testing “solar proton timing,” you are testing “everything weird happening in the sky at once.”

4.3 Cohort Strategy

Main target: UK Biobank

Why use it:

huge sample

brain imaging

cognitive data

actual statistical power


Why not trust it too much:

birth timing precision is limited

gestational timing assignment gets fuzzy

fuzziness weakens any real signal


So UK Biobank is best treated as a discovery cohort, not holy scripture.

Better validation targets

If someone wants to do this properly, use cohorts with tighter birth timing:

ALSPAC

Dunedin

similar birth cohorts with exact dates and longitudinal data


4.4 Exposure Assignment Protocol

For each participant:

1. estimate conception timing from birth date and gestational assumptions


2. mark gestational weeks 22–24


3. query proton flux during that interval


4. calculate cumulative proton fluence


5. attach solar/geomagnetic covariates


6. repeat for control windows



Control windows:

Weeks 10–12

Weeks 30–32


These matter because a fake effect that appears in every window is not a critical-window effect. It is just mush.

5. Outcome Strategy

To keep this from becoming an all-you-can-eat buffet of MRI spaghetti, define a few primary endpoints up front.

Primary outcome candidates

thalamic volume

auditory-region cortical thickness

one prespecified cognitive processing measure


Secondary outcomes

functional connectivity

processing speed

pattern recognition

EEG measures where available

anything exploratory clearly labeled as exploratory


The rule is simple:

Primary outcomes test the idea. Secondary outcomes are there to sniff around, not to rescue failure.


---

6. Statistical Logic

Basic model shape:

Adult Outcome = exposure during weeks 22–24 + geomagnetic covariates + demographic covariates + error

Core tests:

Is there an association?

Is it stronger in the target window than in the control windows?

Does it increase with dose?

Does it survive sensitivity checks?


Nothing fancy required at the prospectus stage. Just enough structure so the thing can be wrong in an orderly way.


---

7. Magnitude Problem

This is the big ugly question and it should be faced directly:

Are these natural field perturbations even strong enough to matter?

That is not a side note. That is one of the main kill conditions.

If the modeled Schumann perturbations from historical SPEs are way below plausible biological relevance, the whole thing mostly dies right there.

That would be a perfectly respectable outcome.

In fact, that is one of the best reasons to post this openly: somebody with better physics chops can swat it fast if the numbers fail.


---

8. Mechanistic Study Arms

These are not required to make the prospectus interesting. They are just the “okay, but how would it work?” branch.

8.1 Magnetite Angle

Biogenic magnetite exists in brain tissue. Maybe it matters here. Maybe it doesn’t.

Testable version: developmentally timed ELF exposure changes later magnetite distribution or electrophysiological response in animal models.

8.2 Calcium-Signaling Angle

Weak-field effects sometimes get routed through calcium-signaling arguments. The literature here is noisy, but it is at least a candidate.

Testable version: organoids exposed to developmentally timed ELF modulation show differences in calcium signaling or synaptogenesis-related expression.

These are candidate mechanisms, not declared truth.


---

9. Falsification Criteria

Project SOLARIS should be considered failed if any of the following happen:

Criterion	Failure Condition

No association	No corrected signal in prespecified primary outcomes
Window non-specificity	Target window is no stronger than control windows
Dose-response failure	More exposure does not map to more effect
Magnitude failure	Field perturbations model out as too weak to matter
Replication failure	Discovery signal dies in better-timed cohorts


This is important because speculative ideas are cheap.
A speculative idea with prewritten kill conditions is at least behaving itself.

Final Statement

Project SOLARIS is a speculative attempt to connect established solar physics, established geophysics, and established developmental neurobiology into one narrow, falsifiable exposure hypothesis. The chain as a whole is not established. That is why the study exists. If the idea is wrong, it should be broken cleanly. If it survives first contact, it becomes worth deeper work. Until then, this is a public-domain object for anyone curious, skeptical, or bored enough to poke it.
